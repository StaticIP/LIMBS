Propositions, Agents and Groups

On the system-wide level, we have three main entities: Propositions, Agents and Groups. This section explores what each of these entities is and how to add them to your simulation.

Propositions

Propositions are any type of information that agents can communicate to each other, which are often the major pieces of information relevant in making a decision. They are split into three subgroups: evidence, which are the factors used to make a decision; actions, which are the results of the decision; and goals, which are the concepts that each agent may want to maximize... or minimize!

Determining propositions is the first major step in understanding the breadth and scope of your model, but don’t worry right now about getting it definitely right, you can always go back and change the list as your understanding of the situation that you are trying to model changes! The goal of this stage is just to start populating the model without having to worry about fine tuning it!

To add propositions to the model, just select the "Propositions" tab at the top of the application, and click the button for the proposition type you want to add. The new proposition will appear on the canvas! Evidence are represented as circles, actions as triangles, and goals as squares. Once you drag and drop them in, you can change their name using the properties bar. Just click on the object, and the right side of the interface should give you all the properties about it in the properties field. Just change the name field to whatever name you’ve given the proposition, and then hit “Apply” and it’s good to go!

Propositions are distinguished visually by both their shape and their colour. Evidence are cyan triangles, actions are medium blue squares, and goals are vibrant blue diamonds.

Susan got out a pen and paper from the top drawer of her desk and started brainstorming. "What factors determine stock booms and busts?"

After a moment’s thought, she determined that the stock buying behaviour of individual people was really what she wanted to model more than the booms and busts themselves.

"What are the factors making up these decisions? Well, the general rule I’ve always heard is that you buy stocks when they are low and sell them when they are high, so STOCK HIGH and STOCK LOW should be pieces of evidence. Realistically, though, you want to buy stocks if they are going up, so GOING UP and GOING DOWN could also be evidence. Same with buyer OPTIMISM and PESSIMISM, at least according to my first year macroeconomics professor!"

"Now for actions, well, that’s easy! BUY STOCK and SELL STOCK! And since the only goal in the whole scheme is to MAKE MONEY, I’ll add that as a goal!"

Susan leaned back in her chair, beaming at the piece of paper on her desk. That model seemed to get closer and closer to completion the more she thought. She smiled.

Agents and Utilities

Agents are the basic power-houses of your simulation. Eventually, you will learn how to customize each agent, giving them their own special way of thinking, making decisions and communicating with others. But don’t get too excited just yet, let’s focus on adding them to the system first!

To add an agent, simply select the "Agent" tab at the top of the application, and click the "HOTCO Agent" button. The new agent will appear on the canvas! To edit the agent’s name, click on the agent on the canvas to select it, then use the name field in the properties panel to update its name. Remember to hit “Apply” when you’re done!

All agents are circles, and the colour of the circle can give you some quick insight into their emotional state. Green means that the agent is feeling good, yellow meaning middling or emotionless, and read meaning bad. The colour can very between these, so a greenish-yellow can mean feeling somewhat good, while an orange could mean feeling somewhat bad.

Utilities are simple, Agent-like components, which also make decisions and communicate, but do so in very strict, not psychological possible ways. They can be used to make the simulation easier to analyse, or to provide some aspects of the simulation that are not the responsibility of a single Agent, like external events, or referendums. Utilities are all identified as magenta octogons.

"Agents," thought Susan, "well, that’s easy! I’ll just put in a few artificial stock traders. That’ll do the trick. But how many?"

She paused.

"Seven. It’s dead wrong, but at least it is a pretty good starting point! Besides, I always liked that number."
As with propositions, don’t worry too much about getting everything about the agent’s just right yet. Focus on getting an appropriate number of agents instead.

Groups

"Do I even have any groups?" Susan thought out loud. "How are these buyers all related?"

She paused again. Maybe modelling wasn’t going to be as easy as she originally thought. At least it isn’t as hard as she thought when she was given the assignment by her adviser less than an hour ago.

"Well, I’ll just have a single BUYERS group for now. If I need any more, I’ll add them later."

The last entity in the LIMBS framework that you need to think about are groups. Groups ask the question “How are agents and propositions organized?” This question can be answered in many ways depending on what you are trying to model. For example, if you are modeling an international negotiation, you may want a group for the negotiators, but you may also want a group for each country involved, so you can model the country giving its negotiators feedback on their decisions.

An interesting thing about groups is that they may overlap. Taking the example of the international negotiators, each negotiator would be part of the negotiator group as well as the group for their respective country. So, each country can overlap with the negotiator group!

Once again, to add a group, simply select the "Group" tab, click the button, and the new group will appear on the canvas. To rename it, just click on the group then use the name field in the properties panel to update the group name. Remember to hit “Apply” to save the changes.
